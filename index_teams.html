Software Team

For Robotic Arm.

Software team is using Gazebo to generate code that will generate a digital robotic arm before the manufacturing and assembly of its physical form. ROS is being used in partnership with Gazebo to create a realistic testing simulation for the robotic arm. The Software team is using C++ and Python as their main programming languages in ROS and Gazebo.

Hardware Team

For Robotic Arm.

The Robotic Arm Hardware Team is in charge of designing and implementing an arm design for the rover. This arm is required to have certain capabilities to complete mission tasks, such as lifting a 5 kg load, type on a keyboard, and screw in a hex screw. In order to complete these objectives, the team will give the arm 6 degrees of freedom, 3 of which will be at the robots wrist near the end effector to give it maneuverability and precision. This arm will be moved by motors acting on worm gears, which were chosen due to the control over rotational speed and torque they provide to the controller. The arm will include an actuated gripper to pick up mission objectives.

Power Systems

Electrical Engineering Team.

The Power Systems team accommodates all sub-systems onboard the rover. Input for the power system is split into the following voltage rails: Communication, Main computer, 12v and 5v logics, Arm and Drivetrain. Providing for the previously listed will be a 6s 24v lithium-polymer battery system protected by a four-way battery management board. Additional features of the system include real-time power monitor and cutoff for all rails.

Mobility & Chassis

Mechanical Engineering Team.

The ME team is in charge of making the chassis and wheels for the rover. The design is being made in Solidworks in order to make an assembly of all the parts. The wheels have a curved cylindrical design with extrusion for friction when traveling more smooth and slippery terrain. For the suspension system, the team is making a traditional rocker-bogie that will help the rover stabilize when climbing over objects. When designing the wheels, the team takes into account the radius and the length of the connecting members so that the front and back wheels do not interact with each other when climbing. The main body of the rover is regarded as a simple 1.2m x 1.2m box that carries all equipment, and serves as the center of mass.

Navigation

Autonomous Navigation Team.

The first goal of the Navigation team is path planning, so we are working on ROS/Gazebo, where we are simulating the movement of the rover in different scenarios and terrains that resemble Mars. The team is also implementing an A-star algorithm that allows the rover to go from point A to B in the shortest path. The second goal is to make sure the rover avoids all kinds of obstacles. For that, we are using a Zed stereo camera that analyses the surroundings of the rover and creates a 3D mesh of the environment in real-time. This data is then used to create another type of navigation map such as an occupancy grid, which allows the rover to safely travel through the free path. Another option we are considering is using a LiDAR sensor to generate a point cloud to identify obstacles. The last goal is to analyze AR tags and find different gates and where the legs would end. To do this, we are working on OpenCV since it offers a variety of ways to scan the AR markers, like using contour detection and finding homography.

Communication

Wireless Communication & Antennas

Communication team is going to set up and configure base stations and antennas to support control signal transmission and video stream reception. The communication system supports 1km range in a non Line-of-Signt environment. 900MHz direcitonal antennas on both the rover and the base station are used to create strong signal.
